Deep Learning World Models Architecture

Two ways to predict a dog — Transformer vs LeWM

A visual walkthrough of why predicting in pixel space and predicting in latent space are fundamentally different bets, and what LeWorldModel gets right by making the second one.

By the Claude editorial team, based on work by · Maes, Le Lidec, Scieur, LeCun & Balestriero · April 2026

Suppose you're watching a cat at time t. You want to predict what frame t+1 looks like after the cat moves right. There are two very different ways to answer this question, and they lead to completely different neural architectures.

The first way: reconstruct every pixel of the next frame. This is what a standard transformer trained as a world model attempts — producing a dense pixel prediction. The second way: skip the pixels entirely, encode the current frame into a compact vector, and predict what the next compact vector should be. That is the core idea behind Joint Embedding Predictive Architectures (JEPAs), and specifically LeWorldModel (LeWM)[1] — the first JEPA that does this stably end-to-end from raw pixels.

The transformer asks "what do the pixels look like?" LeWM asks "what will the state mean?" These are not the same question.

Why pixel prediction is the hard problem

When a cat can become a dog via any number of different transitions — different lighting, different angle, different position — a model trained with mean-squared error in pixel space is forced to average over all of them. The result is a blurry frame that isn't wrong so much as maximally non-committal. The loss surface provides gradient signal, but the signal is noisy because it conflates all the things that didn't happen with the one that did.

This is not a new observation. It is why generative models have historically relied on adversarial training, normalising flows, or diffusion processes — each a different way of avoiding the mean. LeWM makes a more radical move: don't predict pixels at all. Compress first, predict in the compressed space, and let the encoder decide what information is worth keeping.

The three decisions that define LeWM

Encoder/predictor split. A ViT encoder maps the entire frame to a single CLS token vector. The predictor — a small causal transformer — only ever operates in that compressed space. This is why the full model is ~15M parameters and plans up to 48× faster than foundation-model-based world models[1] at inference time.

Action as a first-class signal. In a standard transformer there is no natural place for the action. It would be another token, undifferentiated from the pixel tokens. In LeWM the action embedding is injected into every block of the predictor via Adaptive Layer Norm (AdaLN) — a technique pioneered in DiT[2] for diffusion conditioning. Every attention and MLP operation inside the predictor is shifted and scaled by the action, so the prediction is always conditional on what was done, not just what was seen.

SIGReg instead of an EMA target network. The canonical JEPA collapse problem: if the encoder outputs the same vector for everything, the prediction loss goes to zero for free. Most prior JEPAs — including I-JEPA[3] and V-JEPA[4] — use an exponential moving average (EMA) target encoder to prevent this, which introduces multiple tunable loss terms and a frozen second network. LeWM instead enforces a Gaussian prior on the embedding distribution directly via the Sketch Isotropic Gaussian Regularizer (SIGReg). The result is a single extra hyperparameter, down from six in the only comparable end-to-end baseline.

The comparison is not that transformers are bad and JEPAs are good. Transformers are excellent at predicting the next token in a discrete sequence. The issue is that raw pixel prediction is a particularly hard version of that problem — dense, high-dimensional, and fundamentally ambiguous. LeWM is a reminder that sometimes the right move is to change what you predict, not to scale the thing that's predicting it.
